Project 2
Multigenre Persuasive Campaign
Three artifacts in different genres arguing that the NFL must significantly increase funding for CTE research, targeting two distinct audiences.

Artifact 1 - Open Letter

Complete

Genre: Formal open letter  ·  Audience: NFL executives and team owners

Medium: Linguistic/textual - suitable for publication in a major sports outlet such as ESPN or The Athletic

A four-paragraph letter to Commissioner Roger Goodell and team owners arguing that the league's financial resources create both the ability and the obligation to fund independent CTE research. Primary appeals: ethos and logos, grounded in the Mez et al. (2017) JAMA study and NFL revenue data.

Artifact 2 - Infographic

Complete

Genre: Infographic  ·  Audience: General public and sports fans

Medium: Visual and linguistic - designed for social media distribution (Instagram, Twitter/X, Facebook)

A single-page visual presenting key CTE statistics, a brief disease explanation, affected players, and a call to action. Primary appeal: pathos - the contrast between the NFL's $20 billion in revenue and its research investment is designed to provoke a sense of injustice and motivate sharing.

Artifact 3 - PSA Script

Complete

Genre: Public Service Announcement script  ·  Audience: General public and sports fans

Medium: Linguistic, audial, and visual - intended for broadcast television or online video

A short video script using a fictionalized former player's story to create emotional investment before delivering statistics and a call to action. Works alongside the infographic to reach the same audience through a different emotional and sensory register.

Global Revision
Open Letter - Original vs. Revised
The revision focuses on structure, counterargument, and evidence integration. Both versions will appear side by side in the final portfolio.

What Is Being Revised and Why

Artifact 1

Original artifact: Open Letter to NFL Executives and Team Owners (Project 2)

Reason for selection: The open letter is the most argument-driven artifact and has the clearest opportunities for meaningful global revision. Two weaknesses identified: the letter does not anticipate the audience's likely resistance, and the transition between the science paragraph and the financial argument is abrupt.

Planned Global Revisions
  • Add a counterargument paragraph - acknowledge the NFL's existing concussion protocols and settlement, then explain why those fall short of what independent research funding would accomplish
  • Strengthen transitions - make the logical move from "here is what we know" to "here is what you have the power to do" explicit rather than implied
  • Sharpen the closing - name a specific concrete action rather than a general call to act
  • Side-by-side layout - final website will display original and revised text in two columns so changes are visible at a glance

The reflective statement centers on audience awareness - how understanding who you are writing for changes every decision you make as a writer.

  • Connection to my revision: Adding the counterargument paragraph to the open letter came from thinking harder about my audience and their likely resistance
  • How I will use this going forward: In Biological Sciences and future health fields, writing for patients, physicians, and policymakers all require the same rhetorical thinking I practiced in this course
  • Final version will be at least 1,000 words, Times New Roman 12pt double-spaced

Planned Opening Paragraph

Before this course, I thought of writing as something you did in one voice for one reader - usually a professor grading a paper. What ENC 2135 changed was my understanding that every piece of writing exists inside a rhetorical situation, and that situation determines almost everything: who you are addressing, what genre fits that audience, what appeals will land, and what the writing is supposed to make someone do...

Planned Section Structure

1. The concept: Audience awareness - Define the term and trace when I first encountered it through the rhetorical situation slides in Week 7.

2. How I applied it in Project 2 - Walk through the decision to write three genres for two audiences, with specific examples of how audience changed tone, evidence, and medium in each artifact.

3. How it shaped my revision - The counterargument paragraph added to the open letter came directly from thinking harder about who was reading it and what they would object to.

4. How I will use it in the future - In pre-med and clinical settings, communicating about health topics requires translating the same information for completely different readers: patients need plain language, physicians need clinical precision, policymakers need cost-benefit framing. The rhetorical tools from this course apply directly.
