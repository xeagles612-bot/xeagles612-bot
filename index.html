Roger Goodell, Commissioner
National Football League
345 Park Avenue
New York, NY 10154

Dear Commissioner Goodell and NFL Team Owners,

Every Sunday, millions of Americans watch NFL players perform at the highest levels of human athleticism. What most fans do not see - and what too many players and their families have only learned after it was too late - is the silent, accumulating damage taking place inside those players' brains. Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy, or CTE, is a progressive neurodegenerative disease linked to repetitive head trauma. It has been found in the posthumous brains of more than 300 former NFL players, including Junior Seau, Mike Webster, and Dave Duerson. It cannot be diagnosed in living patients. And the funding allocated to understanding, detecting, and preventing it remains woefully insufficient given the resources and moral responsibility of this league.

The science is not in dispute. A landmark study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association examined 111 donated brains of former NFL players and found evidence of CTE in 110 of them - a rate of 99 percent (Mez et al., 2017). Researchers at Boston University's CTE Center have spent two decades documenting the devastating consequences of the disease: memory loss, impulse control disorders, severe depression, and dementia. Despite these findings, no reliable method yet exists to diagnose CTE in living patients. Without a living diagnostic, players cannot receive timely treatment, families cannot plan, and the league cannot be held medically accountable.

The financial argument is straightforward. The NFL generated over $20 billion in revenue in 2023 alone. The league's existing concussion settlement with former players, while significant in scale, was structured around compensating past harm rather than investing in the science needed to prevent future harm. A dedicated, independently administered CTE research fund would represent a fraction of annual revenue while potentially transforming the medical landscape for every player currently on an NFL roster.

I am not writing to assign blame for what has already happened. I am writing because what happens next is still a choice. The men who play this game deserve the full force of this institution's resources directed at understanding what the game does to their brains. Fund the research that could one day allow a player to be diagnosed while still alive, receive treatment while there is still time, and make genuinely informed decisions about their own health and future.

The players gave everything to this league. The least this league can do is give something meaningful back.

Respectfully,
Nickolas Jason

The science is not in dispute. A landmark study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association examined 111 donated brains of former NFL players and found evidence of CTE in 110 of them - a rate of 99 percent (Mez et al., 2017). Researchers at Boston University's CTE Center have spent two decades documenting the devastating consequences of the disease: memory loss, impulse control disorders, severe depression, and dementia. Despite these findings, no reliable method yet exists to diagnose CTE in living patients. Without a living diagnostic, players cannot receive timely treatment, families cannot plan, and the full scope of the disease cannot be understood.

I recognize that the NFL has already taken steps in this space. The league's concussion protocol and the 2013 settlement with former players represent real acknowledgments of the problem. But a settlement that compensates past harm is not the same as funding the science that prevents future harm. Most of that money went to legal costs and individual claims - not to independent research labs working to develop a living diagnostic test. Acknowledging that CTE is a problem and investing in solving it are two very different things, and so far the league has done far more of the former than the latter.

That gap matters because the science is solvable - but only with sustained, independent funding. The NFL generated over $20 billion in revenue in 2023. A dedicated CTE research fund of even $100 million annually - less than one percent of revenue - would represent a transformational investment in the Boston University CTE Center and similar institutions. It would be structured independently of league interests, administered by leading neuroscientists, and directed specifically at developing a living diagnostic test and identifying treatment pathways.

I am not writing to assign blame for what has already happened. I am writing because what happens next is still a choice. The men who play this game deserve the full force of this institution's resources directed at understanding what the game does to their brains. I am asking the NFL to commit to a dedicated, independently run CTE research fund - a concrete financial commitment, not a protocol revision or a settlement payment, but science funding that outlasts any single legal agreement.

The players gave everything to this league. It is time the league gave something lasting back.

Respectfully,
Nickolas Jason

Reflective Statement

What I learned in ENC 2135 and how I will use it going forward

Audience Awareness as a Rhetorical Skill
Focusing on how understanding who you are writing for changes every decision you make as a writer.

Prior to enrolling in ENC 2135, I believed that writing had a single version - you wrote it, submitted it, and that was it. The notion that a piece of writing could be essentially reorganized in light of a more profound insight into who was reading it was not something I had been previously requested to do. What this course transformed was that I now realize that audience is not a box to be checked on an assignment sheet. The main decision that influences all the others is the genre you write in, the tone you write in, the evidence you emphasize on, and the manner in which you conclude. The most transferable thing I learned this semester has been learning to think rhetorically about audience and this is what I concentrated on when I returned to revise my open letter to include in this portfolio.

My initial real awareness of the concept of audience awareness as an effective tool occurred in Week 7, when we examined the HIV/AIDS Memorial Quilt as a rhetorical artifact. The quilt was not a letter, not a speech, not a petition - it was a tangible item constructed of pieces of fabric that were sewn together by families who had lost loved ones. What was rhetorically effective was that it fitted its audience perfectly. It was placed in Washington D.C., before the Capitol, at a time when policymakers were the target audience that had to be moved. It was an unanticipated and heartfelt genre that a written argument could never be and due to that it succeeded. That exercise was the first time that I considered genre as something that is not a form to be filled in but a choice that is made with a purpose.

I brought that thought into Project 2. I decided to write an open letter to NFL executives because I carefully thought about who these people are and what type of communication they care about. Neither will they be convinced by a post on Instagram. They are involved in formal communication, legal documents, and policy proposals. Their language is an open letter, one that may be published in a big sports paper. In deciding on an infographic to present to the general audience, it was the reverse rationale - this group is not going to read a four-paragraph argument, but they will pause to scroll to a statistic that will shock them. All the genre decisions in the campaign boiled down to one question: what will ever get to this person, considering who he is and where he spends his time?

In revising the open letter to include this portfolio, audience awareness was once again the motivation factor. As I read it back, I have to admit that I had overlooked the most obvious objection the audience would have; that NFL has already done something about this. Concussion settlement. There are protocols. Any executive, who had read my original letter, would have thought of that at once, and since I had never referred to it, the letter was one-sided. The updated version recognizes such efforts that are in place and then justifies why they are not effective enough. That one addition helped to make the whole argument more powerful since it demonstrated to the audience that I knew their side before telling them to switch sides.

Being a Biological Sciences major, I will continue with the rest of my academic and professional life to communicate on complex issues with people who possess very different degrees of expertise. A patient does not require the same explanation that a physician provides. The same framing is not required of a policymaker as it is of a researcher. This skill of recognizing an audience, knowing what they already know and believe, and the selection of the genre and evidence that will actually reach them is not just a writing skill - it is a thinking skill. That is what ENC 2135 provided me with, and that is what I will apply in all the courses and career situations that will occur in the future.
